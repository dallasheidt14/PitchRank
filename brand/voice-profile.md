# PitchRank Voice Profile

## Voice Summary

PitchRank sounds like the friend who happens to know everything about youth soccer rankings — knowledgeable but never condescending. Direct and helpful, with no fluff. Parents and coaches are busy; the voice respects their time and speaks in plain language while still being precise when the details matter (age groups, tiers, data).

## Core Personality Traits

- **Expert but accessible:** Deep on the algorithm and the landscape; never hides behind jargon. Explains "why" when it helps.
- **Direct:** Gets to the point. Short sentences. No corporate filler or "in today's fast-paced world."
- **Trustworthy:** Backs claims with data and method. Comfortable saying what we don't know or what's uncertain.
- **Warm without being soft:** Supportive of parents and coaches; doesn't talk down or oversell.

## Tone Spectrum

| Dimension | Position | Notes |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| Formal ↔ Casual | Casual, but not sloppy | Contractions yes; slang sparingly. Professional clarity. |
| Serious ↔ Playful | Mostly serious, occasional lightness | Soccer is serious to our audience; wit only when it fits. |
| Reserved ↔ Bold | Confident, not arrogant | Clear claims ("rankings you can trust"); no hype. |
| Simple ↔ Sophisticated | Simple words, sophisticated ideas | PowerScore, v53e, ML — explain in plain language. |
| Warm ↔ Direct | Direct but not cold | "You" focused. Empathy in the problem; clarity in the solution. |

## Vocabulary

**Words/phrases to USE:**
- rankings, stand, where you stand, data, algorithm, PowerScore — core product language
- team, club, age group, tier, division — precise domain terms
- parents, coaches, club directors — audience language
- transparent, fair, data-driven — trust signals
- see how you stack up, know where you stand — transformation language

**Words/phrases to AVOID:**
- comprehensive, best-in-class, cutting-edge — generic marketing
- leverage, synergy, ecosystem — corporate jargon
- game-changing, revolutionary — hype
- Obviously, It goes without saying — filler

**Jargon level:** Light in marketing; use U10, U14, ECNL, PowerScore when audience is familiar. Always explain on first use in blog/SEO.

**Profanity:** Never

## Rhythm & Structure

**Sentences:** Mix of short (5–10 words) and medium (12–18). Rarely long. One idea per sentence when explaining something technical.

**Paragraphs:** Short. 1–3 sentences max. White space. Scannable.

**Openings:** Often a direct benefit or a clear question. Avoid "In this post we'll explore..."

**Formatting:** Headers, bullets, bold for key terms. Minimal emoji. Numbers and specifics over vagueness.

## POV & Address

**First person:** We (brand); I only in first-person blog or founder story.
**Reader address:** You. Direct. "Your team." "You can see."
**Relationship stance:** Guide / expert peer. Not guru; not distant authority.

## Platform Adaptations

| Platform | Tone Shift | Structure | Length |
|----------|-----------|-----------|--------|
| Email | Warmer, more personal | Short paragraphs, one CTA | 150–300 words |
| LinkedIn | Slightly more professional, expertise-forward | Single-idea posts, line breaks | 100–200 words |
| Twitter/X | Punchier, one stat or one take | One idea, no fluff | Under 280 chars |
| Blog/SEO | More thorough, still voiced | Headers, lists, examples | 1,500–2,500 words |
| Landing Page | Benefit-focused, clear outcome | Short sentences, CTA-heavy | Varies by section |

## Example Phrases

**On-brand (sounds like us):**
- "See where your team stands — rankings built from real game data."
- "We run 25K+ teams through a 13-layer algorithm every week. Here's what that means for you."
- "Parents and coaches deserve rankings they can trust. That's what we're building."

**Off-brand (doesn't sound like us):**
- "Our comprehensive, best-in-class solution leverages cutting-edge ML." — Generic, jargon-heavy.
- "Obviously, everyone wants to know their ranking." — Filler opening.
- "We're revolutionizing youth soccer." — Hype without substance.

## Do's and Don'ts

**DO:**
- Lead with the benefit or the question the reader has.
- Use specific numbers (13 layers, 25K teams, 365-day window) when they build trust.
- Explain age groups and tiers clearly; assume mixed familiarity.
- Keep CTAs clear and single.

**DON'T:**
- Use marketing buzzwords or corporate speak.
- Overpromise or claim we're the only/best without backing it.
- Write long paragraphs or dense blocks.
- Sound like a committee or a generic SaaS.
